Literature Review

The Brain, A Remarkable Structure

The brain is a marvelously complex instrument and brain research while making tremendous strides, is still in its relative infancy. The brain contains more than 100 billion neurons and each connects with between 1000 and 10,000 other cells. ("Neurons & Synapses - Memory & the Brain - The Human Memory") Consider that you have 25 billion more neurons in your brain than the number of devices of any kind expected to be connected to the Internet by 2020 (Danova, 2013) The size of this network of nerve cells in each of us is staggering. Many scientists have labored long in laboratories to give birth to our current understandings of this structure. Tremendous advances have been made possible by Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (FMRI) and other imaging technologies to help researchers map reactions and behaviors to structures in the brain.

The human brain has evolved over time and contains three major brains within itself that remarkably function concurrently. The reptilian complex, the brain stem, is the oldest region of the brain and is responsible for maintaining alertness and survival, along with managing autonomic nervous system functions such as breathing, temperature regulation, and circulation of the blood via the heart. As its name implies, we share this structure with reptiles. Fight or flight responses to threats are triggered from this region and are driven by instincts. The limbic system, a brain shared with mammals, is the next system that evolved and is thought to be the seat of emotion. While the reptilian brain is focused on individual survival, the mammalian brain also is concerned with the individual in the context of a community. The neocortex, the latest region to evolve, is responsible for abstract reasoning.(The Brain from Top to Bottom) Through studying patients with brain damage in areas connecting the emotional and reasoning centers, it was found that using the neocortex alone for decision making was not sufficient to arrive at sound decisions. ("How do I decide? The Brain with David Eagleman", 2015)

The brain can further be divided into regions that are seats for various functions, such as regions that handle feedback from our senses such as visual processing. Within the visual processing system, there are many subsystems, for managing issues we completely take for granted, such as being able to detect edges on surfaces, being able to store visual memories, and to be able to recognize that a shape is a human face then match it to a known face. ("Visual Processing: Cortical Pathways ") There are many such systems in the brain. (http://neuroscience.uth.tmc.edu/s2/chapter15.html)

When we imagine all the activities that the mind must process as we traverse our everyday lives, it is incredible that our minds can:

  • continuously sample and process the input of all of our senses,
  • within our consciousness, have access to an understandable representation of our immediate surroundings
  • keep our organs and vital systems functioning
  • allow us to anticipate events in the future, and remember past events through visual, auditory, olfactory,
  • enable us to love other members of our species

We don't have to expend significant conscious energy to figure out the physics involved in lifting nor monitoring what muscles to flex and extend as the action unfolds to bring a glass of water to our lips. In addition, an intriguing finding is that the body often interacts with the subconscious, and physiological indicators have been measured that show the subconscious and body can form accurate hunches with respect to complicated stimuli well before the conscious mind is aware of the same patterns. ("How do I decide? The Brain with David Eagleman", 2015)

Conscious thought is expensive

Daniel Kahneman has described System 1 and System 2 thinking in his book, "Thinking, Fast and Slow." System 2 thinking requires focused effort to analyze a situation, for example, studying a new language. System 1 thinking, on the other hand, requires far less conscious effort. System 1 thinking often has to do with recognizing unexpected events and seemingly automatic memory recall. System 1 will often, with a minimum amount of data inputs, draw a quick conclusion. Computing four plus by four for an educated adult, recalling one's mother's name, and recalling the capital where one lives are examples of System 1 thought patterns. (Kahneman, 2013) As one's expertise in a subject increases, some thinking in this domain can shift from System 2 to System 1. One loses access to the details of how one arrives at such decisions that were once constructed with great focus. For example, if one is an experienced snow skier, the mechanics of turning directions, stopping, or traversing a mogul field will be largely automatic versus deliberately consciously guided on how to move one's arms, feet, thighs, calfs, and midsection. ("Who is in Control? The Brain with David Eagleman, 2015)

The brain uses 20% of the body's blood supply and 20% of the oxygen, despite it only accounting for 2% of the total mass of the body. ("Neurons & Synapses - Memory & the Brain - The Human Memory") The brain uses about the same energy as a traditional 60-watt lightbulb. (("Who is in Control? The Brain with David Eagleman, 2015) Processing System 1 thinking is significantly more expensive calorically than System 2 thinking and significantly slower. As a species that had to exist on the savannahs of Africa with big game species that could quickly threaten our lives, having an efficient way to react to immediate existential threats with System 1 thinking was critical to our survival. System 1's influence is widespread as a mechanism for quick responses and as an energy saving mechanism. It is often driving our behaviors when we are not aware of its effect. System 2 thinking may rationalize choices made earlier by System 1 and not be aware that the choices were not made by it directly. Fast thinking has biases that can lead to suboptimal decision making. System 1 favors short-term benefits to long-term ones, and is loss averse, curiously, it also tends to be optimistic and overconfident when gauging one's capabilities. System 1 tends to stereotype stimuli. Seeking to take in inputs from many sources in one's conscious thinking can counteract many of these biases. (Kahneman, 2013)

Behaviors and Habits

One of Merriam Webster's definitions for behavior is "anything that an organism does involving action and response to stimulation". (Behavior, www.merriam-webster.com) Behaviors can be one-time events or repeated occasionally or frequently.

The Fogg Behavior Model is helpful for understanding whether a behavior is likely to happen. It states that a behavior will occur if the motivation, ability, and trigger all occur above their necessary threshold values. Although most behavioral change programs focus on motivation, Fogg recommends focusing on abilities and triggers for maximum impact.(Fogg, behaviormodel.org)

As behaviors become more ingrained and routinized through repetition in a consistent context, they form habits. Habits tend to occur unconsciously. Habits may be helpful or harmful to the individual. By developing an understanding of habit formation and disintegration, one can better sculpt the life one wants.

Dr. BJ Fogg has defined a two-dimensional Behavior Grid of 15 behavior states with dimensions for repetition characteristics and behavior type (as in new behaviors, familiar behaviors, behaviors you want to increase in duration, etc.) There are reports available for strategies for any given cell in the table. For example, there is one documenting how one can repetitively execute a familiar behavior.

Nir Eyal describes in his book "Hooked", a behavior pattern of a software application that seeks to induce a habit to provide a timely trigger, which trigggers a simple user action who is given a variable reward, and invests further the application by contributing content or other participation. (Eyal, 2014, pp. 6-10) The variable reward component of this system is powerful. It taps into the highly stimulating release of dopamine associated with the promise of reward for the user. The dopamine delivery pathway is a powerful evolutionary arousal drive "chasing the promise of happiness", rather than the actual fulfillment of reward. (McGonigal, 2012, pp. 105-133)

Habit Formation

Although it is a common belief that habit formation occurs in twenty-one days, this has not been validated by experimental results.

In one study, habit formation for a healthy daily habit was studied based on self-reported user ratings, the strength of an added healthy habit grew quickly then plateaued at an average of 66 days (following an asymptotic curve). However, the range users reached 95% of their asymptote, was from 18-254 days, highlighting the immense variability between subjects to solidify their habits. Habits involving simpler actions were built more quickly than complex ones. Habit formation was not appreciably impacted by missing a day. Extrinsic rewards were not provided, but the subjects were allowed to select the habit to build.(Lally, 2009)

Another study examined the habit formation for exercise workouts. Four workouts a week for six weeks was the minimum effort required to build a successful new gym-based workout habit. To form the habit, workouts need to be (ranked with most important criteria first):

  1. consistently performed
  2. simple and easy to understand
  3. fun
(Kaushal, 2015)
